
In 18th- and 19th-century [German philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_philosophy "German philosophy"), a _**Zeitgeist**_(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeitgeist#cite_note-1) (German pronunciation: (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German") (https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/6/68/De-Zeitgeist.ogg/De-Zeitgeist.ogg.mp3 "Play audio"); lit. 'spirit of the age'; capitalized in German) is a way of referring to the intellectual, cultural, ethical and political climate of a given [epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoch "Epoch") in [world history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_history_\(field\) "World history (field)"). It is often described as an invisible agent, force, or [daemon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daimon "Daimon") that seems to dominate the characteristics of a particular period.(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeitgeist#cite_note-2) The term is usually associated with [Georg W. F. Hegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel "Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel"), contrasting with Hegel's use of _[Volksgeist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volksgeist "Volksgeist")_ ("national spirit") and _[Weltgeist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weltgeist "Weltgeist")_ ("world-spirit"), although the word itself predates Hegel and was popularized by [Johann Gottfried Herder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Herder "Johann Gottfried Herder") and [Johann Wolfgang von Goethe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe "Johann Wolfgang von Goethe").

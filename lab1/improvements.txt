Based on your current implementation of the hill climbing algorithm for the
  Knapsack problem, here are several improvements you can make to enhance its
  efficiency and effectiveness:


  1. Shift from Recursion to Iteration
  Your current hill_climbing function uses recursion. While elegant, recursion
  in Python can lead to a RecursionError if the search path becomes very long
  (especially as you increase the number of items). Switching to an iterative
  loop (using while or for) makes the algorithm more robust for larger problem
  sizes and slightly more memory-efficient.


  2. Implement Soft Penalties for Constraints
  In your calculate_value function, any solution that exceeds the weight
  capacity returns a value of 0. This creates a "flat" fitness landscape where
  the algorithm cannot distinguish between a solution that is slightly over
  capacity and one that is massively over. By applying a "soft penalty"—where
  you subtract a value proportional to the excess weight—the algorithm can "see"
  a path back toward the feasible region, allowing it to explore the boundaries
  of the capacity more effectively.


  3. Enhance Neighbor Selection (Stochasticity)
  You are currently using Steepest-ascent Hill Climbing, which evaluates every
  single neighbor before moving. While thorough, this is computationally
  expensive.
   * First-choice Hill Climbing: You could move to the first neighbor you find
     that is better than the current one. This often reaches a local optimum
     much faster.
   * Stochastic Hill Climbing: Choosing a random upward move from the available
     better neighbors can help the algorithm avoid getting stuck in predictable
     patterns.


  4. Explore Beyond 1-Bit Flips
  Your generate_candidates function only flips one bit at a time. This limits
  the "reach" of the algorithm.
   * N-bit flips: Occasionally flipping two or three bits at once (a larger
     "jump") can help the algorithm leap across small "valleys" in the fitness
     landscape that a 1-bit flip cannot bridge.
   * Swapping: If the knapsack is full, flipping one bit to 0 always decreases
     value, and flipping another to 1 might exceed capacity. A "swap" neighbor
     (changing a 1 to 0 and a 0 to 1 simultaneously) maintains the item count
     and can find better combinations that simple flips would miss.


  5. Metaheuristic Additions
  To escape local optima (where your algorithm stops because no immediate
  neighbor is better, even if a much better solution exists further away):
   * Simulated Annealing: Allow the algorithm to occasionally accept a worse
     solution. The probability of doing this starts high and "cools down" over
     time. This helps the algorithm "jump out" of local holes early in the
     search.
   * Tabu Search: Keep a short list of recently visited solutions (a "Tabu
     list") and forbid the algorithm from returning to them. This forces the
     search to explore new areas of the map and prevents it from cycling between
     the same few states.


  6. Robust Random Initialization
  Your current initialization logic using np.unpackbits on a uint8 is limited to
  8 bits and specifically sliced for 5 items. As you scale to larger datasets
  (e.g., 20 or 100 items), this approach will fail. Using a more generalized
  method to generate a random bit string of any length will make your solver
  applicable to any problem size.

* Diversity vs. Intensity: Hill climbing is great at "intensity" (refining a
local solution) but weak at "diversity" (exploring different areas of the
solution space). If you find your results aren't improving much even with
many candidates, your random restarts might be landing too close together,
or your search might be getting "trapped" too early.
* The "Plateau" Problem: In discrete problems like Knapsack, you might find
"plateaus"—regions where flipping a bit doesn't change the total value
(perhaps because the bag is full). A basic hill climber will stop here.
Deciding whether to allow "sideways" moves (moving to a neighbor with the
same value) can sometimes help you slide across a plateau to find a new
upward slope.
* Scaling: As you move from 5 items to 50 or 100, the "neighborhood" (the
number of possible 1-bit flips) grows linearly, but the total solution
